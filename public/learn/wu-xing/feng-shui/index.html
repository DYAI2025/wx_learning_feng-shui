The guide in six points

  • Wu Xing—五行, wǔxíng—is a fivefold framework of dynamic relations, not a list of chemical substances.
  • Wood is associated with east and spring; Fire with south and summer; Metal with west and autumn; Water with north and winter; Earth often occupies the centre or seasonal transitions.
  • Colour correspondences are traditional categories, not universal interior “dosages.”
  • Feng Shui is not one method: form-focused and compass-focused approaches use Wu Xing differently.
  • No room has a fixed phase merely because it is called a bedroom, office or kitchen.
  • Use physical evidence first, then treat Wu Xing as an interpretive layer if desired.
Historical context

Where Wu Xing fits into Feng Shui

The Great Plan chapter of the Book of Documents describes Water, Fire, Wood, Metal and Earth through characteristic actions—descending, rising, bending and straightening, yielding and changing, sowing and harvesting. Later correlative traditions linked these categories to seasons, directions, colours, tastes, governance and other domains.

Feng Shui drew on this broader cosmological vocabulary. In practice, Wu Xing becomes one way to describe relationships: support through 生 (shēng, generating) and regulation through 克 (kè, controlling). It is not the whole of Feng Shui. Site form, orientation, time, lineage and purpose also matter.

Diagram literacy

North-up maps and traditional south-facing diagrams

A diagram position is not automatically a compass direction. Modern maps normally place north at the top. Some traditional Chinese cosmographic layouts are organised from the perspective of an observer facing south: south is in front or at the top, north behind or below, east to the left and west to the right.

Both layouts can communicate the same correspondences if clearly labelled. The crucial rule is not to mix a north-up map, a south-facing cosmogram and a relation-only five-node cycle without explanation.

Design rule: A generating or controlling cycle can be rotated because it shows relational order. A directional Feng Shui diagram cannot be rotated casually; its compass convention must be explicit.
Method variation

Feng Shui is a family of approaches, not one formula

Academic overviews commonly distinguish broad form-focused and compass-focused approaches while warning that terminology and school boundaries vary across texts, regions and lineages.

Form-focused approaches

Read terrain, watercourses, enclosure, exposure, approach routes and visible configuration. Wu Xing may describe forms, but the starting point is the physical setting.

Compass-focused approaches

Use orientation, directional sectors, calendrical or time models and a Feng Shui compass. Wu Xing relations are applied according to the selected method.

Three Harmonies and related lineages

Often associated with relationships among landform, direction and water. Historical labels and teaching sequences are not perfectly uniform.

Three Cycles, Flying Stars and Eight Mansions

Later compass-based systems use different inputs and rules. Flying Stars usually adds time-dependent charts; Eight Mansions uses another directional classification.

A general guide should explain distinctions, not issue predictions.

The same phase can play a different role depending on whether a method reads terrain, direction, trigram, number, period or chart relationship. Combining rules from different schools without provenance produces false precision.

Responsible application

How to read rooms without turning symbolism into pseudoscience

Popular advice such as “add Water in the north” or “use red in the south” is too blunt. A safer sequence starts with the building and method before choosing a symbolic intervention.

  1. Observe physical reality. Check daylight, glare, temperature, ventilation, moisture, acoustics, circulation, fire risk and accessibility.
  2. Define the spatial question. Is it site exposure, entrance flow, work focus, rest, storage or another concrete use?
  3. Name the approach. Form reading, compass analysis and time charts should not be conflated.
  4. Verify inputs. Orientation, construction period, floor plan and calculation rules may be required.
  5. Use Wu Xing relationally. Describe support, regulation or contrast as traditional symbolic logic, not a causal guarantee.
  6. Choose reversible, safe changes. Symbolism never overrides codes, health advice or evidence-based environmental design.

Bedroom

Prioritise darkness control, quiet, thermal comfort and safe circulation. “Bedroom equals Water” is not a universal classical rule.

Workspace

Prioritise task lighting, ergonomics, distraction control and storage. Wood-like growth or Metal-like clarity are metaphors, not diagnoses.

Kitchen

Prioritise fire safety, extraction, hygiene and workflow. Literal heat does not justify claims about luck, relationships or health.

Entrance

Prioritise visibility, weather protection, accessibility and a clear route. Traditional methods may additionally consider orientation and approach.

Historical practice

Traditional applications—and what can safely be said

Historical scholarship places Feng Shui within a long field of site selection, dwelling and burial divination, orientation, cosmology and popular religious practice. The term “feng shui” became common later than several earlier labels, and the further back one goes, the harder it is to separate Feng Shui neatly from broader cosmology and divination.

Applications commonly discussed

  • selecting and evaluating sites for settlements, buildings, temples and graves;
  • reading landforms, water, enclosure, exposure and approach;
  • orienting buildings, entrances and important positions;
  • using compass and time systems in particular lineages;
  • describing relationships through phase correspondences and cycles.
Not established by these traditions alone: that a coloured object changes cortisol, that a crystal loses “energy” in glass, that a star combination guarantees illness or theft, that a water feature creates wealth, or that a BaZi chart must always be combined with a building chart.
Claim safety

Limits and common misconceptions

“Feng Shui is five-colour decorating.”

No. Colour is one correspondence among many. Site, form, direction, time and method can be more important.

“Every direction has one fixed life outcome.”

No. Directional meaning depends on the selected system; wealth, romance or illness claims are often overstated.

“Wu Xing is ancient physics.”

No. It is a traditional correlative and process framework. Internal coherence is not empirical validation.

“A cure works because the cycle says so.”

The cycle explains symbolic relations inside the tradition. It does not demonstrate a physical or medical mechanism.

“Period 9 rules apply to all Feng Shui.”

No. Period models belong to particular time-based lineages.

“More phase objects mean more balance.”

No. Counting objects is a modern simplification. Context and method matter.

Glossary

Essential terms

Wu Xing · 五行 · wǔxíng

A fivefold traditional framework used to classify and relate processes. “Five Phases” is often clearer than “Five Elements,” though translations vary.

Generating relation · 生 · shēng

Wood supports Fire, Fire supports Earth, Earth supports Metal, Metal supports Water and Water supports Wood. These are mnemonic traditional models, not physical mechanisms.

Controlling relation · 克 · kè

Wood regulates Earth, Earth regulates Water, Water regulates Fire, Fire regulates Metal and Metal regulates Wood.

Form-focused approach

A broad label for methods that begin with visible terrain, water, enclosure, exposure and built form.

Compass-focused approach

A broad label for methods using orientation, directional sectors, calendrical models or a Feng Shui compass.
